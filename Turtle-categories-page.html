Green sea turtle

There are five varied species of sea turtles in Sri Lanka's waters, including the green sea turtle (Chelonia mydas). Due to its threatened status, it is protected by Sri Lankan law.

The coastal waters near Sri Lanka are home to green sea turtles, which eat algae and seagrasses. On numerous beaches in Sri Lanka, including Rekawa, Kosgoda, and Kalametiya, they have been observed to lay their eggs.

Despite conservation efforts, green sea turtles in Sri Lanka are in danger from several factors, such as habitat loss, pollution, and poaching. Local organizations are working to safeguard turtle nesting areas, promote sustainable tourism methods, and increase public awareness of the value of sea turtle conservation.

To ensure both the survival of the green sea turtles and the wellbeing of the marine ecosystem, it is crucial to keep track of and safeguarding Sri Lanka's green sea turtle populations.




The Hawksbill turtle

The Hawksbill turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata) is one of five sea turtle species found in Sri Lanka's waters. It is a severely endangered species and is protected by Sri Lankan law.

The coastal waters near Sri Lanka are home to hawksbill turtles, which eat sponges, algae, and other invertebrates for food.

Despite conservation efforts, Hawksbill turtles face several threats in Sri Lanka, including habitat loss, pollution, and poaching. Local organizations are working to protect turtle nesting sites, raise awareness about the importance of sea turtle conservation, and promote sustainable tourism practices.

Overall, it is critical to continue monitoring and protecting Hawksbill turtle populations in Sri Lanka to ensure their survival and the health of the marine ecosystem. They are known to lay their eggs on several beaches in Sri Lanka, including Rekawa, Kosgoda, and Kalametiya.


Oliver ridley turtle

In Sri Lanka's waters, there are five varied species of sea turtles, including the Olive Ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea). It is a threatened species and is protected by Sri Lankan law.

Olive Ridley turtles can be found in coastal waters around Sri Lanka, where they feed on a variety of marine organisms such as jellyfish, shrimp, and crabs. On numerous beaches in Sri Lanka, including Rekawa, Kosgoda, and Kalametiya, they have been observed to lay their eggs.

The loss of habitat, smog, and poaching are just a few of the threats that Olive Ridley turtles in Sri Lanka face despite conservation efforts. Sea turtle nesting sites are being protected, sea turtle conservation is being emphasized, and sustainable tourism is being promoted by local organizations.

Overall, it is important to continue monitoring and protecting Olive Ridley turtle populations in Sri Lanka to ensure their survival and the health of the marine ecosystem.


Leatherback turtle

The Leatherback turtle (Dermochelys coriacea) is one of five sea turtle species found in Sri Lanka's waters. It is a critically endangered species and is protected under Sri Lankan law.

Leatherback turtles can be found in coastal waters around Sri Lanka, where they feed on jellyfish and other soft-bodied organisms. They are known to lay their eggs on several beaches in Sri Lanka, including Rekawa, Kosgoda, and Kalametiya.

Despite conservation efforts, Leatherback turtles face several threats in Sri Lanka, including habitat loss, pollution, and poaching. Local organizations are working to protect turtle nesting sites, raise awareness about the importance of sea turtle conservation, and promote sustainable tourism practices.

Overall, it is critical to continue monitoring and protecting Leatherback turtle populations in Sri Lanka to ensure their survival and the health of the marine ecosystem.




Loggerhead turtle

A species of sea turtle found in the waters off Sri Lanka is the loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta). The peak nesting season for loggerhead turtles in Sri Lanka is from October to February.

Due to a number of threats, including habitat loss, egg poaching, and unintentional fishing gear capture, the loggerhead turtle population in Sri Lanka has been declining.

Sri Lanka has implemented a number of conservation initiatives to safeguard loggerhead turtles, including the creation of marine protected areas, enforcement of laws against turtle harvesting and egg poaching, and promotion of sustainable fishing methods. Sand beaches, seagrass beds, and coral reefs are just a few of the habitats where loggerhead turtles can be found.

They are known for their extensive migration and can cover thousands of kilometers between their feeding and nesting grounds.

The length and weight of an adult loggerhead turtle can both exceed three feet.
